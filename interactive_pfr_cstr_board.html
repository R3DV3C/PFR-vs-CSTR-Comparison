Secondary Treatment: Activated Sludge Overview

Activated sludge is an aerobic suspended-growth process where return sludge (biomass) is "activated" by aeration in the reactor. It stabilizes organic content and removes nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus).

  • Purposes: Biological stabilization of organics; nutrient removal.
  • Process types: Suspended growth (aerobic, anaerobic), attached growth, combined systems.
  • Variants: Conventional, extended aeration, SBR, oxidation ditch, step/tapered aeration.

Two reactor configurations apply to activated sludge:

SystemTypeFlow Mode
Plug FlowLong rectangular channelContinuous, unidirectional
Completely MixedBasin with mixers or diffusersContinuous mixing

1. Concept & Hydraulic Behavior

In PFR-based activated sludge, mixed liquor flows through a long, narrow aeration channel. Biomass and substrate move as discrete plugs with negligible axial mixing, ensuring a gradient of microbial activity along the length.

Equation: $$\frac{dC}{dx} = -\frac{k}{u}C \quad\Rightarrow\quad C(x)=C_0 e^{-k x/u}$$ where HRT θ=L/u.

2. Process Parameters

ParameterDesign Role
Mixed Liquor Suspended Solids (MLSS)Biomass concentration; influences reaction rate.
Dissolved Oxygen (DO)Maintain >2 mg/L for aerobic metabolism without backmixing.
Return Sludge Rate (RSR)Controls biomass age and F/M ratio.
Flow Velocity (u)Determines HRT and plug integrity.

3. Performance Indicators

  • BOD Removal: >95% removal typical due to uniform aerobic conditions.
  • Sludge Age: Controlled by RSR; affects nitrification in high-rate channels.
  • Effluent Clarity: Enhanced by sequential substrate depletion.

4. Design Considerations

  • Baffle placement to prevent short-circuiting.
  • Control diffuser spacing and blower capacity for uniform DO.
  • Provision for sludge return line and wasting port.

5. Advantages & Disadvantages

  • Advantages: Effective BOD removal; escalates nitrification in stages.
  • Disadvantages: Complex channel layout; higher head loss; challenging control at low flows.

6. Applications

  • Conventional activated sludge with plug flow aeration basins.
  • Oxidation ditches (circular PFR variant).
  • Tapered aeration basins for variable BOD loading.

1. Concept & Hydraulic Behavior

In CSTR activated sludge, mixed liquor is uniformly mixed in a tank by mechanical aerators or diffused air. Effluent concentration equals mixed liquor concentration, simplifying control of biomass and DO.

Mass balance: $$Q(C_0-C)=VkC \Rightarrow C=C_0/(1+kθ)$$ where θ=V/Q.

2. Process Parameters

ParameterDesign Role
MLSSTarget 2000–4000 mg/L for conventional plants.
Sludge Volume Index (SVI)Indicator of settleability in final clarifier.
F/M RatioBalanced by sludge wasting to maintain biomass age.
Aeration RateMatches oxygen demand of mixed liquor.

3. Performance Indicators

  • Effluent BOD: Typically 20–30 mg/L for conventional activated sludge.
  • Settleability: Measured by SVI, crucial for clarifier performance.
  • Process Stability: Robust to load fluctuations due to mixing.

4. Design Considerations

  • Tank geometry: circular or rectangular with adequate depth (3–5 m).
  • Mixing intensity: prevent dead zones, but avoid shear damage to flocs.
  • Sludge recycle/pumping: control for return activated sludge (RAS) and waste activated sludge (WAS).

5. Advantages & Disadvantages

  • Advantages: Simple to operate, easy sampling, tolerant to influent swings.
  • Disadvantages: Larger reactor volume, less efficient nitrification gradient, potential short-circuit if mixing uneven.

6. Applications

  • Conventional activated sludge basins in municipal WWTPs.
  • Extended aeration plants (longer HRT in CSTR configuration).
  • SBR systems operate as batch CSTRs with timed exchange.

Selection Guidelines

PFR: Ideal when influent characteristics are stable, space is linear, and high nutrient removal is required in stages.

CSTR: Preferred for variable loads, simplicity, and where mixer infrastructure exists.

Hybrid Configuration

Use a CSTR (equalization basin) upstream to buffer influent, followed by a PFR polishing channel to maximize removal. This combination stabilizes flow/loads and delivers high effluent quality.
